## Background

The Iterated Prisoner's Dilemma (IPD) is a classic problem in game theory that models the tension between cooperation and competition in repeated interactions. In each round, two players independently decide to either **cooperate** or **defect**, and their payoffs depend on the joint action.

## Question

Can RL agents discover behaviors like **generosity, forgiveness, and kindness** — the long-known keys to long-term cooperation — purely from rewards, when matched against classical IPD strategies (Tit-for-Tat, Grim Trigger, Pavlov, Random, etc.)?

## Approach

I implemented a tournament environment for the IPD and trained reinforcement-learning agents to optimize their strategies against a pool of classical agents. Both the action policies and the resulting interaction dynamics were tracked over training and across opponents.

## Outcome

The RL agents converged on cooperative-leaning policies, learning to exploit defectors while sustaining mutual cooperation against retaliators. The experiment shows that the principles that make Tit-for-Tat-family strategies successful can emerge from reward-driven learning alone.

## Stack

Python, PyTorch, custom IPD environment, classical strategy implementations.
